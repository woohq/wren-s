# VESTIGE — Moral Complexity in Worldbuilding

*No pure villains. No easy heroes. Everyone has their reasons.*

**This is a core design constraint**, equal in importance to "no ad-libs" and "characters first." Every faction, every god, every NPC, and every threat in Vestige must pass the moral complexity test.

---

## The Seven Principles

### 1. Origin Wounds
Every faction carries a trauma that predates the current fight. The Iron Holds' isolation isn't stubbornness — it's survival memory. The Rootwalkers don't steal — they survive in a world that wrote laws for people who stay put.

### 2. Internal Logic
Each faction's worst actions make sense within their own experience. Trace the reasoning. If the reader can't follow the logic, the faction isn't complex enough.

**The test:** Write a monologue where a true believer defends their faction's worst action — not with propaganda, but with genuine reasoning. If it isn't at least a little persuasive, rewrite.

### 3. Internal Divisions
No faction is a monolith. Every group contains:
- **Hardliners** who want more aggression
- **Moderates** who want compromise
- **The exhausted** who just want it to stop
- **Opportunists** who profit from the status quo
- **Idealists** who remember what the faction was supposed to be

The most human moments often come from arguments *within* a faction.

### 4. Outside Powers With Mixed Motives
When cultures or factions interact, nobody's purely predatory or purely victimized. The Sunward Courts trade with the Iron Holds because they want the ore AND because they admire the craft. Both things are true. The gap between stated reasons and real reasons is where story lives.

### 5. Colonial Echoes
Build founding injustices into history. Decisions made by distant powers about people they didn't consult. The consequences cascade across generations. Nobody alive caused the original wrong, but everyone alive lives with it.

### 6. Information Asymmetry
Each side sees a different version of reality, and each version is partially true. In Vestige, this has a unique dimension: **the god sees both sides. The mortal sees one.** The tension between divine knowledge and mortal perspective is the mechanical expression of this principle.

### 7. Costly Peace
Every possible resolution demands something genuinely painful from someone. If peace is easy and one side is just refusing it, we've written a villain, not a faction.

## Applied to Vestige's First Era

### The Iron Holds vs The Sunward Courts

**Not a conflict of good vs evil. A conflict of competing legitimate needs.**

**Iron Holds perspective:** The southerners want our ore, our steel, our craft. They come with trade agreements that look fair but always benefit them more. Their scholars study our forges and take the knowledge home. They call us primitive while wearing armor we made. We've survived for centuries by keeping our secrets. Opening up means losing what makes us *us*.

**Sunward Courts perspective:** The northerners sit on the richest ore deposits in the known world and refuse to trade fairly. Their isolationism isn't principled — it's paranoid. We bring literacy, medicine, connection to the wider world. We're offering them a future. Their refusal isn't noble independence — it's fear dressed up as tradition.

**Both are right. Both are incomplete.**

**The internal divisions:**
- Iron Holds: Young smiths who've seen southern steel and want to learn vs masters who consider it a betrayal of tradition
- Sunward Courts: Senators who genuinely want cultural exchange vs senators who want to control the ore supply

**The colonial echo (future era):** When the Sunward Courts eventually establish a "trade outpost" in Iron Holds territory, staffed by southern merchants and guarded by southern soldiers — was that cooperation or occupation? Depends who you ask.

### The Rootwalkers and Everyone Else

**The Rootwalkers are not noble savages or romantic wanderers.** They're people with a sophisticated knowledge system (riversongs), genuine cultural strengths, and also genuine flaws.

**Other cultures' perspective:** They don't build, don't contribute to infrastructure, don't pay taxes, and their "River Tongue" network moves stolen goods. They benefit from the roads and bridges settled peoples build while refusing to help maintain them.

**Rootwalker perspective:** The roads weren't built for us. The taxes fund armies that harass us. The "stolen goods" are often our own possessions confiscated at border checkpoints that didn't exist when we were here first. We don't build because the river teaches that nothing permanent is worth more than what moves through it.

**The exhausted middle:** Rootwalker elders who are tired of the road and wonder if settling would be so bad. Settled farmers who grew up playing with Rootwalker children at market and don't understand why their government treats them as criminals.

### The Ashfield Awakening

**Even the threat isn't purely evil.** A god born from accumulated fear — centuries of travelers projecting dread onto the volcanic plains. Nobody intended to create this thing. Every culture contributed to it through their shared unease. When it manifests, it's not malicious — it's a newborn consciousness shaped entirely by terror, trying to understand itself through the only lens it has.

**The moral complexity:** Destroying it means destroying something that was accidentally created by the collective fear of every culture. It's everyone's fault and nobody's fault. A character who figures this out faces a choice: kill a newborn god, or try to reshape it by changing what people believe about the Ashfield.

## The Player-God's Unique Position

The player sees all sides. The mortal sees one. This creates the game's deepest moral tension:

- You KNOW the Iron Holds' secrets are worth protecting AND the Sunward Courts' knowledge would genuinely help them
- You KNOW the Rootwalkers aren't thieves AND they do benefit from infrastructure they don't maintain
- You KNOW the Ashfield god is dangerous AND it was accidentally created by the same cultures trying to destroy it

**What do you do with knowledge that makes every side partially right?**

That question is Vestige's thesis. The answer is written in the accumulated choices across dozens of runs. And there is no correct answer — only the one you keep choosing.

---

## The Writing Test

Before any faction, god, NPC, or threat is finalized, it must pass:

1. **Can you write a sympathetic monologue from their perspective?** If no, deepen them.
2. **Does their worst action follow from their experience?** If not, fix the logic chain.
3. **Do they have internal disagreements?** If they're unified, they're not real.
4. **Would a player who allies with them feel justified?** And would a player who opposes them ALSO feel justified? If both answers aren't yes, rewrite.
5. **Does peace between them and their rival require genuine sacrifice from both sides?** If only one side needs to change, you've made a villain.
