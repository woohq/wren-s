# VESTIGE — Endgame States

*There is no final boss. There is only the world you built.*

---

## The Overarching Dynamic

There is no single world-ending villain. The threat is escalation itself — rational actors making rational decisions that compound into potential mutual destruction. Every faction arms itself for legitimate reasons. Every escalation makes the others feel more threatened. The gods amplify what mortals feel. The divine ecosystem mirrors mortal anxiety back with amplification.

The player-god's job across eras isn't to defeat something. It's to build enough bridges, trust, and shared identity that when the final era's pressure peaks, the factions choose de-escalation over destruction.

The tools for de-escalation were built (or not built) across every previous era. Alliances. Trust. Mixed cultures. Shared philosophy. Trade that benefits both sides. Every relationship, trade route, and shared song is a thread that might hold the world together. If those threads don't exist, there's nothing to hold.

---

## The Endgame Is Not Scripted

The final state EMERGES from the accumulated state of every faction, every relationship, every resolved or unresolved tension across all eras. The game doesn't pick an ending. The world arrives at one.

---

## Possible Final States

### Dominance
One faction achieved supremacy. Could be military conquest, economic absorption, or cultural assimilation. The dominant faction's nature determines the world's character.

Not a "good" or "bad" ending — depends entirely on which faction and how they got there. An Iron Holds military empire is a different world than a Senate trade hegemony. Both have costs traceable to the player's choices.

### Alliance (with cost)
Two or more factions found common ground — at the expense of a third. The most historically common outcome. The allied factions prosper. The marginalized faction becomes a permanent underclass, a resistance movement, a diaspora, or they migrate entirely.

The player can trace exactly when and how the marginalized faction got left behind.

### Genuine Peace
The rarest outcome. Requires bridges built across MULTIPLE eras — trade that benefited both sides, philosophical exchange, mixed-culture generations, trust earned through repeated cooperation. Even then, fragile. Held together by the accumulated weight of everything the player built.

This isn't utopia. It's hard-won and imperfect. Some groups are still unhappy. Some wounds haven't healed. But the guns aren't pointed at each other.

### Mutual Destruction
The Cold War outcome. Nobody wanted it. Everybody caused it. Escalation reached its logical endpoint. The factions destroyed each other and the world enters a dark age.

New cultures emerge from the rubble. The player-god persists into a broken world. Everything they built is gone. They start rebuilding from almost nothing — but with knowledge of what went wrong.

The most devastating playthrough. The most motivating restart.

### External Threat Unaddressed
While the factions escalated against each other, something else grew. The Ashfield god. Something from the Oldwood. Something across the sea. The factions were so focused on each other that the real danger blindsided them all.

The irony ending: they had enough power to save the world. They were too busy pointing it at each other.

### Fragmentation
No faction wins. No faction loses. The world breaks into small pieces. City-states. Tribal zones. The Brackish model everywhere. Nobody has enough power to threaten anyone, but nobody has enough power to protect anyone either.

Safe from apocalypse. Vulnerable to everything else.

### The Specialist Ending
The player spent every life in one faction. That faction is magnificent — rich, detailed, deeply shaped by centuries of divine attention. Everything else is a question mark. The summary shows your people in full detail and everyone else as what-ifs.

The ambiguity IS the ending. You built something beautiful. You have no idea what it cost.

### The Default
The player couldn't engage. Died too young, too often. The world played out its default timeline. Resentful compromises. Endemic problems. Stagnant cultures. Nobody's fault except the god who wasn't there.

The quietest and heaviest ending. Not tragedy — mediocrity. A world that could have been more.

---

## What Determines the Outcome

The final state is a function of:

1. **Faction power levels** — military, economic, cultural, divine support
2. **Faction relationships** — alliances, rivalries, dependencies, grudges
3. **Trust** — accumulated through player-driven cooperation across eras
4. **Unresolved tensions** — anything not addressed keeps escalating
5. **External threats** — anything ignored keeps growing
6. **The god ecosystem** — which deities are empowered, which concepts have materialized
7. **Cultural bridges** — mixed communities, shared philosophies, trade networks
8. **The player's domain** — what the god became affects what tools they have at the end

No single factor decides. It's the interaction of all of them. That's why two playthroughs never end the same way.

---

## The Final Summary

The game ends with a chronicle. Not a score. Not a grade. A history.

For each faction: what they became. How the player shaped them (or didn't). Key moments traced to specific choices.

For the player-god: their domain. Their name. The lives they lived. The parts of the world they touched and the parts they never reached.

The last line of every playthrough:

*Here is the world you built. One life at a time. Across centuries.*

*Was it enough?*
