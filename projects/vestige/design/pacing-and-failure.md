# VESTIGE — Pacing, Failure, and Vessel Strategy

*The world doesn't need a hero. It needs someone who shows up.*

---

## The Default Timeline (No Player Intervention)

Without a god's intervention, the world doesn't freeze — it declines. The First Era's tensions don't resolve. They compound.

**The Southern Offer:** Defaults to resentful compromise (~10 years). Trade exists but nobody trusts it. Neither open nor closed. The worst of both.

**The Ash Cough:** Defaults to endemic Stage 2 (~20 years), Stage 3 (~40 years), Stage 4 (~60 years). Nobody investigates the source. Nobody discovers unworship. The plague becomes the background of existence. Cultures adapt around it rather than solving it.

**The Philosophical Split:** Defaults to tribal camps. No dominant philosophy. The Iron Doctrine, the Proposition, and the Song become identity markers instead of living ideas. People stop asking "what's the answer?" and start asking "which side are you on?"

**The era transition:** Fires after ~60-80 years regardless. The world that emerges is stagnant, weakened, and defined by problems nobody addressed. Not apocalyptic — just less. A world that got older without getting wiser.

**This IS the bad ending.** The game doesn't announce it. It shows you the summary: here's what became of everything while you were busy dying. The weight of it is the hook. The player puts the game down, thinks about it, and starts over.

---

## Failure as the Best First Experience

A player who fails — who dies young repeatedly, never engages with the big events, never shapes the world — has the most powerful motivation to replay. They SAW the world suffer. They know what happens when nobody helps. Now they have KNOWLEDGE, which is the only real progression.

The restart carries no mechanical advantage. The god is still forgotten. The world resets to its initial state for a fresh playthrough. But the PLAYER knows:
- Which roads have bandits
- When the plague starts
- What the Southern Offer means
- How the Ashfield god works
- Which traits help survival
- Where to find allies

Knowledge is the god's memory. The Fear and Hunger principle completes: death taught you how to live.

## Life Length Variance

Not every life is a full arc. That's the point.

**Short lives (death at 5-20):**
- Childhood + maybe early adolescence
- The player learns the world's geography, dangers, culture
- Minimal world impact — but small personal spawns (grieving families, minor ripples)
- Expected in runs 1-3

**Medium lives (death at 20-40):**
- Full adolescence + early adulthood
- Attunement awakens. First real combat. First real choices.
- Engages with 1-2 medium events
- Expected in runs 3-6

**Long lives (death at 40-70+):**
- Full life arc. Major events. World-shaping decisions.
- Multiple relationship arcs resolve
- The character leaves a significant mark
- Expected in runs 5+

**The era transition fires based on accumulated resolution, not run count.** A player who lives one legendary 70-year life might resolve more than a player who lived five short ones. But the short-life player learned things the long-life player didn't — they know what the world looks like from the bottom.

## Vessel Selection as Strategy

Choosing who to embody is choosing who to care about — and who to abandon.

**The specialist:** Keeps choosing the same faction. Ten lives in Thornwall. They know every street, every family, every forge. Their Iron Holds are rich, detailed, deeply shaped. Everything else is the default timeline. Their god becomes a god OF something specific — the forge, the mountain, the craft.

**The generalist:** One life here, one there. Touches everything, masters nothing. Their world is more even but shallower. No faction deeply loved, no faction deeply neglected. Their god becomes a god BETWEEN things — connection, balance.

**The strategist:** Deliberately avoids a faction. Not from neglect — from calculation. "The Senate needs to fail for the Rootwalkers to thrive." A god making political decisions about who to abandon. Dark. Interesting. The game should let you feel the weight.

**The accidental:** Doesn't think strategically. Picks whoever seems interesting. Their world is chaotic, unpredictable, human. Probably the most realistic god — one who acts on impulse and lives with the results.

All valid. All produce different domain crystallizations. All produce different worlds.

## The Roadwarden Lesson

The optimal path still leaves someone hurting. This is not a design flaw — it's the thesis.

- Helping the Iron Holds means the Rootwalkers didn't get your attention
- Curing the Ash Cough means the Southern Offer went unanswered
- Resolving trade means the plague advanced another stage
- You cannot be everywhere. You are one god in a world that needs twelve.

**The best possible outcome:** The factions you invested in thrived. The ones you didn't had default outcomes — some okay, some bad. The world bears your fingerprints. Some are healing. Some are scars. You can trace every scar to a choice you made with the best information you had.

That's not a happy ending. It's an honest one. Honest is what stays with you.

---

## The Final Summary

At the end of a full playthrough (all eras), the game shows:

**For each faction/culture:**
- What became of them
- How much the player influenced their trajectory
- Key moments that shaped their fate (traced to specific player choices)

**For the player-god:**
- Domain that crystallized
- Name the world gave you
- The lives you lived, summarized
- The parts of the world you never touched

The factions you loved will make you proud or break your heart depending on whether your interventions helped. The factions you ignored will show you what happens when a god looks away. Neither feeling is comfortable. Both are earned.

**There is no score. There is no rating. There is only: here's what you built, across centuries, one life at a time. Was it enough?**

The answer is always no. And the wanting-it-to-be-yes is why you play again.
