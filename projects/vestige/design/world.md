# VESTIGE — World Systems

*The world doesn't wait for you. It has its own momentum.*

---

## Core Principle

The world is alive independent of the player. Factions pursue agendas. Threats escalate. Cultures evolve. Technology advances. All of this happens between lives whether the player participated or not. Your actions create DELTAS from the world's natural trajectory. The accumulated deltas across all runs create your unique version of history.

---

## Cultures

### Cultures Are Alive

Cultures are not labels. They're living, blending, fracturing systems that evolve through contact.

**A culture has:**
- **Origin values** — what it believed before contact with others
- **Naming pools** — sets of names appropriate to the culture
- **Available archetypes** — which life-paths are common here
- **Trait tendencies** — which environmental traits develop here
- **Relationship norms** — how people relate (honor-based, transactional, communal)
- **Worship tendencies** — which gods/concepts are revered
- **Technology level** — what tools and knowledge are available
- **Contact history** — who they've met and what changed

### Cultural Evolution

When two cultures meet, the contact produces change:

```
Culture A + Culture B → Contact Event
  ├→ Trade: exchange of technology, ideas, goods
  ├→ Merging: a hybrid culture forms (Bridge Folk)
  ├→ Resistance: purist faction splits off (Old North)
  ├→ Dominance: one absorbs the other (the absorbed goes underground)
  ├→ Schism: the culture fractures into subcultures
  └→ War: conflict over incompatible values
```

**Example evolution:**
```
Northern Clans (Era 1): honor, oral tradition, ancestor worship, iron-working
  ↓ Contact: Southern Empire trade route opens (Era 2)
  ├→ Old North (Era 3): honor, oral tradition, isolationist, anti-trade
  ├→ Bridge Folk (Era 3): honor-lite, literate, trade-connected, pragmatic
  └→ Exiles (Era 3): neither, wandering, adaptive, rootless
```

Each branch is a full culture with its own naming pools, values, archetypes, and events. The player's past-life choices might have CAUSED the schism.

**Neither pure nor merged is "better."** Old North has internal coherence and strong identity but is isolated and rigid. Bridge Folk are connected and adaptable but have lost something — their children don't know the old songs. Both are valid. Both create different stories.

## Factions

Factions are organizations with goals, resources, and reach. They exist across cultures and eras.

**A faction has:**
- **Type:** kingdom, religion, guild, military order, criminal network, scholarly institution
- **Goal:** what they're working toward (expansion, knowledge, profit, divine mandate, survival)
- **Resources:** military strength, economic power, political influence, magical capability
- **Territory:** where they operate
- **Leadership:** who runs it (may be a past character's descendant)
- **Relationship map:** allies, enemies, neutral parties
- **Worship implications:** a powerful faction generates Devotion for its leaders

### Faction Advancement

Between runs, factions pursue their goals:
- An expansionist kingdom pushes its borders
- A merchant guild seeks trade monopolies
- A religious order spreads its faith
- A criminal network infiltrates institutions

**If the player did nothing:** the strongest/most aggressive faction wins by default. History follows the path of least resistance.

**If the player intervened:** the delta changes the outcome. Supporting the weaker side, assassinating a general, introducing a technology, converting a key figure — each action modifies the trajectory.

### Faction Relationships

Factions interact:
- **Alliance:** shared goals, mutual benefit. Unstable if goals diverge.
- **Rivalry:** competing for the same resource/territory. Can become war.
- **Parasitism:** one faction lives off another (criminal guild and the kingdom it infests).
- **Dependency:** one faction needs the other (kingdom needs the merchant guild's trade routes).

The player can influence these relationships across runs.

## Eras

Eras are not fixed time periods — they're defined by the dominant tension in the world.

### Era Progression

Each era has:
- **Defining tension:** what the world is struggling with
- **Active threats:** what's growing if unaddressed
- **Technology level:** what tools and knowledge exist
- **Cultural state:** which cultures are active, which are fading
- **Faction landscape:** who holds power

**Example era sequence (one possible history):**

| Era | Defining Tension | If Player Engages | If Player Ignores |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1: Foundation | Civilizations forming, gods establishing | Shape which cultures dominate | The strongest survive by default |
| 2: Contact | Cultures meeting, first conflicts | Broker peace or pick a side | Wars of consolidation, strongest wins |
| 3: Plague | A magical corruption spreading | Trace the source, find a cure | Continent-wide endemic disease |
| 4: Empire | One faction dominant | Influence from within or rebel | Imperial overreach, internal rot |
| 5: Collapse | The empire falls | Guide the transition | Chaos, power vacuum, dark age |
| 6: Rebirth | New order emerging | Shape what grows from the rubble | Whatever survives becomes the new normal |

**The order and nature of eras depends entirely on player actions.** If you prevent the plague, Era 3 might be an Age of Exploration instead. If you prevent the war, Era 2 might be an Age of Trade. The world generates its own history; the player's involvement changes which history it generates.

## Threats

Threats are world-scale dangers that escalate on a timer.

### Threat Stages

Every major threat progresses through stages if unaddressed:

```
Stage 1 — Whisper:    Faint signs. Easy to address if someone investigates.
Stage 2 — Rumor:      Regional impact. Harder to ignore. Refugees appear.
Stage 3 — Crisis:     Major cities affected. Armies can't solve it. Requires understanding.
Stage 4 — New Normal: The threat IS the world now. Society adapts around it.
```

**The player who catches it at Stage 1** has a side quest. **The player who faces it at Stage 3** has a world-defining crusade. **The player who ignored it entirely** lives in a Stage 4 world where the threat has become the background of existence.

### Example Threats

**The Hollow Scourge (magical plague):**
- S1: Strange illness in border villages. Sick animals in the forest.
- S2: Entire region affected. Villages abandoned. Refugees spread it.
- S3: Major cities falling. Source traced to a corrupted god. Armies useless.
- S4: Continental. Civilization restructured around it. Plague-resistant cultures thrive. Others extinct.

**The Warlord's March (military conquest):**
- S1: A charismatic leader unifying nomadic tribes. Distant thunder.
- S2: Border raids. Small kingdoms falling. Refugees.
- S3: Major invasion. Multiple kingdoms threatened simultaneously.
- S4: Empire of conquest. Resistance is underground. The world is occupied.

**The Silent God (divine awakening):**
- S1: Strange dreams reported across a region. Scholars notice a pattern.
- S2: A concept god is gaining awareness. Worship is forming accidentally.
- S3: The god manifests. Its domain conflicts with existing gods. Competing influence reshapes worship patterns — believers waver, prayers redirect, old rituals feel hollow.
- S4: The pantheon is restructured. Some old gods lose worshippers and fade to the margins. The new god reshapes the divine landscape.

## World Persistence Across Runs

### What Carries Forward

**Tier 1 — Personal (every life):**
- NPCs you knew persist (age, have children, remember or forget)
- Your character becomes memory → story → legend → forgotten
- Artifacts you created/found exist in the world
- Relationships echo (descendants carry forward feelings about you)

**Tier 2 — Local (notable lives):**
- Buildings you built/destroyed persist
- Settlements you defended survive / ones you didn't may fall
- Apprentices you trained become masters, establishing traditions
- Discovered routes and resources become known
- Local faction reputation changes

**Tier 3 — Regional (exceptional lives):**
- War outcomes change borders
- Installed leaders rule (well or badly)
- Brokered peace or started conflicts persist
- Spread or suppressed religions reshape culture
- Created artifacts of power exist in the world

**Tier 4 — World-shaping (legendary lives):**
- Started a new religion
- Slayed or failed to slay a major threat
- United warring factions into an empire
- Accidentally created a new threat (demon lord, cursed bloodline, weapon that shouldn't exist)

### The Cascade System

A single decision can cascade across eras:

**The seed:** Save a starving child in run 2.
**The growth:** The child becomes a military leader in run 5.
**The bloom:** The leader conquers three kingdoms in run 7.
**The consequence:** Your run 9 character lives under the tyrant you saved as a child.

**Attribution:** The game tracks causal chains. The player discovers the connection through narrative — finding old texts, hearing stories, recognizing names. The game NEVER says "you caused this." The player figures it out.

### The Between-Runs Simulation

After each death, the world advances:

1. **Apply direct consequences** from the character's life (authored chains)
2. **Advance faction agendas** based on current power levels
3. **Escalate/resolve threats** based on current stage + player intervention
4. **Age NPCs** — some die, some have children, some change
5. **Cultural evolution** — contact events, schisms, merging
6. **Technology advancement** — if conditions were met
7. **Generate new state** — new NPCs born, new tensions forming
8. **Record legacy** — what the character did, who remembers

## Geography

### World Structure

The world has regions, each with distinct geography, climate, and cultural identity.

**A region has:**
- **Terrain:** mountains, coast, forest, plains, desert, tundra
- **Climate:** affects available resources, building styles, threats
- **Settlements:** cities, towns, villages, outposts, ruins
- **Points of interest:** dungeons, shrines, natural wonders, ancient sites
- **Active factions:** who controls or contests this region
- **Active threats:** what's dangerous here
- **Culture(s):** who lives here and how

### Dungeons and Dangerous Places

Not all combat happens in the open. Dungeons are authored locations with:
- **Layout:** rooms/areas with connections
- **Inhabitants:** creatures, NPCs, traps
- **Treasure:** equipment, artifacts, knowledge
- **History:** why this place exists, who built it, what happened here
- **Atmosphere:** authored prose creating tension and mood

A dungeon is a life event: "You discover the entrance to the Old Warren." The player can choose to enter or not. Inside, a series of encounters (combat, puzzle, discovery) unfold through authored text + card combat.

## Technology

Technology advances across eras based on conditions:

| Discovery | Requirements | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Iron-working | Access to ore + smithing culture | Better weapons/armor cards |
| Writing | Settled civilization + scholarly tradition | Knowledge persists, events change |
| Printing | Writing + mechanical knowledge + political will | Information spreads faster, revolutions possible |
| Gunpowder | Alchemy + military pressure | New weapon cards, warfare transforms |
| Magical engineering | High World attunement + scholarly tradition | Enchanted items, constructs, wards at scale |
| Medicine | Bond attunement + scholarly tradition | Wound cards heal faster, plagues easier to address |
| Navigation | Coastal culture + astronomy + shipbuilding | New regions accessible, trade routes |

**The player can accelerate, redirect, or suppress discovery.** Guide a scholarly character toward a breakthrough, and it happens in their era. Suppress a dangerous discovery, and it goes underground — resurfacing later in a more dangerous form.

**Technology doesn't appear uniformly.** One region might have printing while another is pre-literate. The spread depends on trade, conquest, and cultural exchange.
