# VESTIGE — Core Loop

*The minute-to-minute player experience, from god-awakening through dozens of runs.*

---

## The Macro Loop (across runs)

```
AWAKEN → SELECT VESSEL → LIVE A LIFE → DIE → SUMMARY → WORLD ADVANCES → SELECT VESSEL → ...
```

Each iteration of this loop is one "run." A run is 30-90 minutes. The world persists between runs. The god grows across runs. The player's knowledge accumulates forever.

---

## First Run: The Awakening

### Minute 0 — The God Wakes

The god interface. Dark. Sparse. Not a place — a perspective. Fragments of sensation. You were something once.

> *You don't remember waking. You don't remember sleeping. You don't remember anything — and yet there's a shape where memory should be. Like a word on the tip of a tongue that was never yours.*
>
> *Below — if "below" means anything to something without a body — there is a world. You can feel it the way the blind feel sunlight: warmth without detail. Lives flickering like candle flames. Brief. Bright. Gone.*
>
> *Three of them are new. Just born. They burn differently from the others — something in them is... open. Unfinished. Like a door left ajar.*
>
> *You could step through.*

The player sees three candidates. First run = minimal information:
- A child. Somewhere cold. Feels strong.
- A child. Somewhere warm. Feels bright.
- A child. Somewhere crowded. Feels sharp.

No stats. No traits listed. Just impressions. You choose on instinct.

### Why the first run is simple

The god is weak. Weak god = less information = simpler choice = natural onboarding. The player isn't overwhelmed because the GOD can't see much yet. The fiction justifies the tutorial.

Systems introduced in run 1:
- ✅ Text choices (life events)
- ✅ Basic relationships (family, one friend)
- ✅ Mortal power awakening (attunement discovered)
- ✅ First combat (small deck, basic enemies)
- ✅ Death and between-runs summary
- ❌ Worship power (character too ordinary)
- ❌ Vows (too early in progression)
- ❌ Divine intervention (god too weak)
- ❌ World persistence details (nothing to compare to yet)

By run 3, most systems are active. By run 5, everything is in play.

## The Life (within a single run)

### Phase 1: Childhood (minutes 1-15)

**The world is small.** Home, family, street, neighborhood. The prose is intimate and warm — even in a harsh world, childhood has moments of safety.

**What happens:**
- 3-5 childhood events drawn from pools filtered by archetype + place + culture. Event pools include both root events and spawned consequence events from prior runs. See [event-chains.md](event-chains.md).
- Each event offers 2-4 choices that feel personal, not strategic
- Choices invisibly accumulate attunement affinity (brave → Body, curious → World, cautious → Void, observant → Sense, kind → Bond)
- 1-2 relationships form: a family bond deepens, a friend is met
- The world's larger tensions appear at the edges — overhead conversations, distant smoke, a parent's worry

**What the player learns:**
- How to read and make choices
- Who their character is (through the choices they make)
- The tone and texture of this world
- The first hints that something larger is happening

**No combat. No power system. Just a childhood.**

**Example childhood events:**
- "Your mother is arguing with someone at the door. You can hear her voice — the sharp one she uses when she's afraid, not angry. Do you: listen from the stairs / go back to bed / walk out and stand beside her?"
- "The other children are playing Kingfall in the square. Two teams, a wooden crown, and a lot of bruises. Seri waves you over. Do you: join Seri's team / challenge the biggest kid to single combat / watch from the wall and figure out who's going to win?"

### Phase 2: Adolescence (minutes 10-25)

**The world gets bigger.** You leave home, or the world comes to you. The tone shifts — consequences are real now.

**What happens:**
- 4-6 events, increasingly complex. Trait-gated choices appear.
- Relationships deepen through shared experiences (the trial, the secret, the fight)
- A mentor may appear (relational trait source)
- Cultural events reveal faction tensions, religious dynamics, political undercurrents
- **The Awakening:** mortal power manifests for the first time through narrative

**The Awakening (mortal power discovery):**

Not a menu. Not a class selection. A SCENE that reveals your attunement through what happens to your body:

**Body attunement:**
> *The man's hand is around your throat. You can feel your heartbeat in your temples, your wrists, the backs of your knees. And then you feel something else — a heat that isn't heat, a pressure from inside, as if your blood has decided it will not be stopped. Your hand comes up and closes around his wrist, and his grip breaks like a rotten branch. You didn't know you could do that. Neither did he.*

**World attunement:**
> *The fire is spreading too fast. The water bucket is empty. You reach for something — not the bucket, not the door, something else. Something in the air itself. And the air listens. The flame bends, coughs, and dies. Smoke curls from your fingertips. You smell ozone. The world just did what you asked, and you don't know how to ask again.*

**Void attunement:**
> *They're looking for you. You can hear their boots, their breathing, their torchlight scraping the walls. You press yourself into the corner and wish — desperately, completely — to not be here. And then you're not. You're still standing in the corner. But the torchlight passes over you like water over a stone. They don't see you. You don't understand how. You barely exist right now, and it's the safest you've ever felt.*

**Sense attunement:**
> *It happens at the market. One moment you're buying bread; the next, the world cracks open. You can hear the baker's heartbeat. You can feel the woman three stalls down hiding something in her coat. You can taste the iron in the air from the smithy two streets away. Everything is too loud, too bright, too much. You drop the bread. The baker asks if you're alright. You're not. You're hearing the entire city breathe.*

**Bond attunement:**
> *Seri falls. Not far — just off the wall, just onto the cobblestones — but you hear the crack and something in you reaches out. Not your hands. Something deeper. You feel the break in her arm like it's your own bone, and then you feel it... mend. Not completely. But the shattered edges find each other, pressed together by something you're doing without knowing how. Seri stares at her arm. Then at you. "What did you do?" You don't know.*

**First combat encounter.** Simple. Manageable stakes. 3-4 card deck:
- Strike (basic attack)
- Block (basic defense)
- One attunement card (Power Strike / Flame Bolt / Shadow Step / etc.)
- Maybe one equipment card if they have a weapon

The player learns the card system when they can afford to make mistakes.

### Phase 3: Adulthood (minutes 20-55)

**Everything opens up.** This is the meat of the run.

**Systems now active:**
- Mortal power training (new cards, proficiency upgrades)
- Equipment acquisition (each piece = cards in deck)
- Deep relationship events (betrayal, confession, shared crisis)
- World events touching the character's life directly
- The god's accumulated knowledge creating dramatic irony (you KNOW the threat is coming; the character doesn't)
- Potentially: worship beginning if the character becomes notable
- Potentially: vows available if mortal power reaches Focused stage

**The pacing rhythm:**

Like Slay the Spire's map, the player navigates through life events with branching paths. Some are safe (relationship scenes, training, craft). Some are dangerous (combat encounters, political entanglements, exploring ruins). Some are world-events that happen TO you regardless.

The balance: **roughly 60% narrative events, 30% combat encounters, 10% world events.** The player should feel like they're living a life that happens to include fights, not playing a combat game with story breaks.

**Relationship peaks:**

By mid-adulthood, relationships hit critical moments:
- The childhood friend asks something you can't give
- The mentor reveals they were wrong about something fundamental
- The rival does something that forces you to see them differently
- The lover has a secret that changes everything

These are the moments the player remembers between runs. Not the combat. The people.

**The carry-weight decision:**

Equipment management happens naturally — you find a legendary greatsword, but it adds 3 cards to your deck (Cleave, Heavy Swing, Overhead Smash). Your deck is now bigger, which means you draw your key cards less often. Do you keep it? Or stick with the simple sword that adds only Slash and keeps your deck lean?

This is the Slay the Spire principle: **more isn't always better. A focused deck beats a loaded one.**

### Phase 4: The Peak and the Fall (minutes 45-70)

**Late life.** If the character survived this long, they're powerful. The deck is deep. The relationships are rich. The stakes are highest.

**What happens:**
- A major confrontation: the world-scale threat, the political crisis, the personal reckoning
- The choices here plant cascade seeds that will grow across future runs
- Combat encounters are genuinely dangerous — enemies are strong, wounds are likely, death is possible
- The character may take a vow (if they haven't already) for the power needed to face the final challenge
- The character's worship power may peak — if they're a known hero/leader, worship cards are in the deck

**The death:**

It comes. Always. Even if the character survives to old age, they die. The question is how:
- In combat (sudden, possibly mid-sentence)
- Of old age (a quiet scene, the character reflecting on their life)
- Of sacrifice (choosing to die for something — the most powerful seed-planting moment)
- Of consequence (a wound from years ago finally catches up, a betrayal comes due, a vow's cost arrives)

Death is not a fail state. It's a transition. The life was the play. The death is the final move.

## The Between (transitions across runs)

### The Summary

The god-perspective returns. The prose shifts — more distant, more panoramic. You're not IN a life anymore. You're above it.

> *Kira lived 47 years. She was a Body-attuned swordswoman of the Old North. She forged the blade Ashmark. She defended Thornwall from the southern advance. She lost her left hand to the Hollow Scourge and fought on. She never found love. She never learned what the dreams meant. She died in her sleep, and Thornwall mourned for a week.*

The summary shows:
- **Life overview** — how long, where, what they were, what they accomplished
- **Motivations** — fulfilled or unfulfilled
- **Relationships** — who survived, who didn't, who was changed
- **World impact** — specific things that changed because of this character's actions
- **Legacy** — how the character is remembered (or forgotten)

### Time Passes

The world simulates forward 5-50 years (depending on narrative needs):
- Factions advance their agendas
- Threats escalate or resolve
- NPCs age, die, have children
- Cultural evolution continues
- Technology may advance
- The character's legacy either grows or fades

The player sees key changes:
> *In the 30 years since Kira's death: The Hollow Scourge has reached Stage 2. The Southern Empire has consolidated the eastern territories. A new god has been born — the Blade Saint, worshipped by soldiers who carry swords forged in Thornwall's style. Kira's forge still stands. Her apprentice runs it now. He named his daughter after her.*

### Vessel Selection

The god interface returns, clearer now (the god is slightly stronger):
- More candidates visible (4-5 instead of 3)
- More information per candidate (traits visible, family structure, location context)
- Some candidates connect to past runs (born in Thornwall, descended from an old NPC, living in the shadow of a past character's legacy)

The player chooses. The next life begins. And now they KNOW things the character doesn't.

## The Long Arc (across many runs)

### Runs 1-3: Learning

The god is weak. Vessels are ordinary. The player is discovering the world — which factions matter, where threats are growing, how the power system works. Deaths come fast (some runs might be 15-20 minutes if the character dies young). Each death teaches something. The Fear and Hunger principle: knowledge is the only progression.

### Runs 4-7: Influencing

The god has enough power to see more during vessel selection. Divine interventions become available (expensive, limited). The player starts seeing consequences from early runs — the child they saved, the faction they supported, the threat they ignored. The world is visibly shaped by their past choices.

### Runs 8-12: Shaping

Major world-changing actions become possible. The god has enough worship to grant powerful blessings. The player can guide vessels toward uniting kingdoms, confronting major threats, creating world-altering artifacts. But unintended consequences stack up. Every solution creates new problems.

### Runs 13+: The God's Own Story

The world knows about the player-god. Other gods react. Factions pray to you (or curse you). Past characters are legends. The question shifts from "how do I survive?" to "what kind of world am I building?"

**The domain crystallizes.** Across all these runs, the god's nature has been taking shape. The game tracks what you've consistently chosen:
- Protected others → mercy/protection domain
- Conquered → dominion/war domain
- Sought knowledge → wisdom/foresight domain
- Built communities → hearth/home domain
- Created art and beauty → creation/inspiration domain
- Destroyed threats at any cost → destruction/sacrifice domain

The domain isn't announced. It emerges. NPCs start describing you in ways that reflect it. Your worship power shifts to match. The god interface changes aesthetically. By run 15-20, you know what you are — and you can either embrace it or fight it.

### Endgame: The Name

At some point — when the domain is crystallized, when the worship is strong enough, when the world has been shaped enough — the god earns a name. Not chosen by the player. Given by the world.

The name reflects what you became. It's the game's final statement about who you are.

## Pacing Principles

1. **Every life must have at least one moment the player remembers.** A relationship beat, a hard choice, a desperate combat. If a run ends and the player can't name one moment, we failed.

2. **Combat is punctuation, not the sentence.** Fights should feel like crises within a life, not the reason the life exists.

3. **The between-runs summary should make the player feel something.** Not just "here's what changed." A moment of reflection. Weight.

4. **Early runs should end fast.** Don't protect the player. Let them die young. They'll learn faster and care more about survival in later runs.

5. **Late runs should feel heavy.** A character who's survived 60 years has a rich deck, deep relationships, and a lot to lose. Their death should feel like a real loss, not a reset button.

6. **The world should surprise the player.** Not everything is the player's fault. Sometimes things happen that nobody caused. The world is alive independent of the god.
