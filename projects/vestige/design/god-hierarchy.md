# VESTIGE — The Divine Hierarchy

*Worship is gravity. Not good. Not evil. It just exists.*

---

## The Five Tiers

Divinity isn't binary. It's a spectrum, and beings exist at every point on it simultaneously. The tiers coexist — they don't consume each other. A river and an ocean are both water but don't compete.

### Tier 1 — Reputation (barely divine)

The local healer everyone trusts. The blacksmith whose blades are suspiciously good. The bandit everyone fears. They don't know they have power beyond the ordinary. They just seem... more.

**Power:** Slight edge. Luck, timing, presence that fills a room. Nothing they could identify as supernatural.
**Source:** Being in people's minds. A few dozen to a few hundred people thinking about you regularly.
**Awareness:** None. They think they're just good at what they do.
**Prevalence:** Dozens exist at any given time. Most never realize.

### Tier 2 — Historical Figures (actively empowered)

People whose deeds are known widely enough that collective awareness grants real abilities. A war hero who fights harder when people are watching — not morale, Devotion. A scholar with insights that come suspiciously easy because thousands of students invoke their name.

**Power:** Genuine supernatural edge in their domain. A warrior's reflexes sharpen beyond training. A healer's patients recover faster than medicine explains.
**Source:** Hundreds to thousands of people knowing your name and associating it with something specific.
**Awareness:** Some notice. Most rationalize.
**Prevalence:** A handful per era. They shape events.

### Tier 3 — Powers (representing something bigger)

Kings, high priests, cult leaders, legendary figures. They represent nations, faiths, movements. Their power is significant and specifically shaped by what they represent.

**Power:** Clearly supernatural. A beloved king's kingdom prospers in ways economics can't explain. A feared warlord radiates dread that breaks enemy formations before contact.
**Source:** Representing a collective identity. The power comes from the ROLE, not just the person.
**Awareness:** Fully aware. They feel it. Some use it wisely. Some are consumed by it.
**Prevalence:** One to three per major faction. The player's vessel could reach this tier in a long life.

### Tier 4 — Deities (reciprocal relationship with worshippers)

Gods worshipped by cultures that give back. Not distant — transactional. The culture prays, the god provides. The forge-folk revere craft, a forge deity emerges and their steel genuinely improves. The Rootwalkers honor the river, a river spirit keeps the floods predictable.

**Power:** Domain-specific but significant. Can manifest (partially), grant blessings, answer prayers, protect territories. Bound to their worshippers — if the culture changes, the god changes.
**Source:** Active, sustained worship from a culture or significant population. The relationship is a marriage, not a hierarchy.
**Awareness:** Fully sentient. Personality shaped by how they're worshipped.
**Prevalence:** Several per era. They interact with each other — alliances, rivalries, indifference.

### Tier 5 — Concept Gods (born from an era's defining truth)

The Deep, The Burn, The Drift, The Silence, The Weave. These exist because their concepts exist. Fire doesn't need prayer to burn.

**Power:** Vast but impersonal without worship. Worship gives them face, personality, involvement. An unworshipped concept god is background radiation. A worshipped one is a character.
**Source:** The concept itself existing. Worship is optional but changes the god's nature.
**Awareness:** Variable. The Deep thinks in millennia. The Burn thinks in sparks. Some are barely aware. Some are profoundly so.
**Prevalence:** Tied to fundamental concepts. New ones can be born when an era's defining truth is strong enough — an era of heavy war births a war concept god.

**Born from eras:**
- Era of founding → gods of earth, fire, water, death, connection (the original five)
- Era of war → a war god materializes
- Era of plague/fear → a fear god materializes (the Ashfield god)
- Era where the Forge Council rises → a flame/craft god strengthens
- Era of trade → a commerce god begins to form

## Key Principles

**The tiers don't consume each other.** A Tier 4 deity doesn't eat Tier 2 historical figures. A concept god doesn't overwhelm local worship gods. They operate at different scales.

**Worship is neutral.** Like gravity, it holds things together and sometimes things fall. It creates communities, sustains cultures, empowers protectors AND tyrants. The system isn't the problem. What people DO with the system can be.

**The player-god starts at Tier 1** and works their way up through the game. They begin as barely a whisper. Where they end depends on how many lives they guided and how famous those lives became.

**Moving between tiers isn't guaranteed.** Most Tier 1 beings never reach Tier 2. Most Tier 2 never become Tier 3. The jump requires sustained, growing belief directed at you specifically. And each tier brings new burdens — a Tier 3 power is bound to their role, a Tier 4 deity is bound to their worshippers.

---

## The Player-God's Unique Position

The player is a Tier 4 or 5 god who FELL to Tier 0 (below reputation — completely forgotten). They're climbing back. But they're climbing through an unusual method — not gathering worshippers directly, but guiding vessels whose deeds generate worship that flows back to the god.

This means the player's tier at any given moment reflects the accumulated fame of every life they've guided. A player who guided a legendary king gained a lot. A player whose vessels all died young gained almost nothing.

The player's tier determines:
- How much they see during vessel selection
- How many divine interventions they can afford
- Whether other gods notice them
- Whether the world's ending threat can be addressed
