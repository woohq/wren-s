# VESTIGE — Era Transitions

*How the First Era ends depends on what the player did. What comes next depends on what grew from the cracks.*

---

## First Era → Second Era

The First Era ends when its defining tensions resolve — or fail to resolve. Three major axes determine the shape of the Second Era:

### Axis 1: The Southern Offer (Trade vs Isolation)

**If trade opened freely:**
The road exists. Southern goods flow north. Northern ore flows south. Within a generation, Thornwall has a southern quarter. Young smiths read about metallurgy. Old masters watch and grieve. The Forge Council splits — not violently, but the cracks widen. Some masters retire rather than vote on whether to write down the sacred techniques.

*Second Era result:* A blended northern culture emerges — literate, trade-connected, prosperous. But a traditionalist faction retreats to the deeper mountains, calling themselves the True Forge. They keep the old ways. They don't trade. They consider Thornwall fallen. Two northern cultures where there was one.

**If trade was rejected:**
Thornwall closed the passes. The Senate built a trade post at the mountain's base anyway. Young smiths slip down at night. A black market forms. The Forge Council's authority erodes not from outside pressure but from internal disobedience.

*Second Era result:* Thornwall is intact but brittle. The trade post has grown into a small town — half southern, half northern dissidents. It has no official name. People call it the Foot. The Forge Council pretends it doesn't exist. Everyone knows it does.

**If trade was negotiated conditionally:**
The seventeen-clause treaty holds for a generation. Ore goes south, medicine comes north, nobody enters the forges. Then a mixed-culture child is born in the trade quarter and the walls start to dissolve.

*Second Era result:* A gradual blending that nobody planned. The treaty is technically still in effect. Nobody enforces it. A new generation has grown up speaking both languages and finding the old arguments tiresome. This is the most stable outcome — and the one most likely to produce something genuinely new.

**If the player didn't engage:**
The NPC-decided compromise pleased nobody. Trade exists but is resentful. Both sides feel cheated.

*Second Era result:* Simmering tension. Neither open nor closed. The worst of both — the costs of trade without the trust, the isolation without the purity.

### Axis 2: The Ash Cough / Ashfield Awakening

**If treated (Bond healers):**
The death rate dropped but the disease persists. A class of trained fear-drainers emerges — Bond-attuned healers who specialize in the Ash Cough. They're respected and pitied. The work damages them. Some become patients themselves.

*Second Era result:* An endemic disease. Society adapts. The fear-drainers become a recognized profession — and a political force, because everyone depends on them. The Ashfield god continues growing slowly. The problem is managed, not solved.

**If investigated (Ashfield explored):**
The player discovered the god is a newborn breathing plague without malice. This knowledge spreads unevenly. Scholars debate it. The Ash Tenders say they've been saying this for years.

*Second Era result:* An intellectual crisis. If the god isn't malicious, is killing it murder? If the plague is breath, not weapon, is quarantine imprisonment? The philosophical divide deepens. Some factions want to destroy the god. Others want to communicate with it. Ossa becomes a controversial figure — was she right all along?

**If quarantined:**
Thornwall sealed. The infected died isolated. The healthy survived behind walls. Trust between the forge quarter and the upper town shattered.

*Second Era result:* Thornwall physically survived but socially fractured. The upper town and the forge quarter are effectively separate communities sharing a mountain. The walls built for the plague are still standing. Some are literal. Some aren't.

**If Rootwalker knowledge applied:**
The elder sang the old songs. The Ashfield wasn't always feared. The concept of unworship entered the conversation. Unworship: the deliberate redirection or withdrawal of belief to weaken a worship-dependent god. Not destroying — transforming. Change what people believe about something and you change what it is.

*Second Era result:* A radical theological movement emerges. If worship creates gods, can un-worship unmake them? Can you change a god's nature by changing the stories? The Ash Tenders and certain Rootwalker elders form an unlikely alliance. The Senate wants to study it. The Forge Council doesn't trust anything that requires changing your mind instead of building something solid.

### Axis 3: The Philosophical Split

The Three Answers — Iron Doctrine, Consul's Proposition, Song of the Cracked Stone — don't stay philosophical. They become political.

**The Iron Doctrine** hardens into northern conservatism. Craft as moral framework. You are what you make. If you don't make things, your opinions about things are worth less. This philosophy serves the Forge Council's authority — but also produces genuine artisans who take pride in work well done.

**The Consul's Proposition** evolves into Senate interventionism. We understand the problem, therefore we should lead the solution. Knowledge as authority. This serves the Senate's expansion — but also produces genuine scholars who advance medicine, engineering, and law.

**The Song of the Cracked Stone** becomes Rootwalker resistance philosophy. Movement as answer. You cannot own the river. The settled world's problems come from settling. This serves Rootwalker identity — but also produces genuine wisdom about adaptability and the danger of rigidity.

Each philosophy has genuine value AND serves someone's power. That's what makes them feel real.

## What the Second Era Looks Like

The Second Era's defining tension depends on the combination of First Era outcomes. Some possibilities:

**Trade + Treated Plague + Iron Doctrine dominant:**
A prosperous but conservative north. Trade enriches everyone but the old masters feel sold out. The True Forge faction in the deep mountains becomes a symbol of resistance. The managed plague creates a permanent healthcare dependency. The Second Era tension: prosperity vs identity. What did we lose by gaining?

**Rejection + Investigated Plague + Unworship movement:**
An isolated but philosophically radical north. The trade post (the Foot) becomes the most interesting place in the world — where ideas cross borders even if goods don't. The unworship movement creates the first serious challenge to how gods work. The Second Era tension: can you change the nature of the divine? And who gets to decide?

**Conditional Trade + Quarantine + Consul's Proposition dominant:**
A structurally stable but emotionally scarred world. The treaty holds. The quarantine walls hold. Everything holds. But the trust is gone. The Second Era tension: order without trust. The Senate's knowledge-as-authority philosophy fills the vacuum, but authority without trust is just control.

**No engagement + Rootwalker knowledge + Song dominant:**
The world moved on without the player's input. But the Rootwalker philosophy spread — the Song of the Cracked Stone became an anthem. People started walking. Settled communities saw an exodus of young people who'd rather be free than safe. The Second Era tension: what happens when a generation decides the old structures aren't worth maintaining?

## New Cultures That Might Emerge

Depending on outcomes:

- **The True Forge** — Iron Holds purists who rejected trade and retreated to the deep mountains. Oral tradition only. No outsiders. The most skilled smiths in the world, making weapons for nobody.

- **Bridge Folk** — the mixed-culture children of the trade quarter. Speak both languages. Belong to both worlds and neither. Pragmatic, adaptable, distrusted by purists on both sides.

- **The Foot** — if trade was rejected, the unofficial town at the mountain's base. Half southern traders, half northern dissidents. A culture of defiance and commerce.

- **The Fear-Drainers' Guild** — if the plague was treated, the Bond-attuned healers who specialize in Ash Cough. A profession that becomes a political force.

- **The Unworshippers** — if the Rootwalker knowledge path was taken, a radical theological movement. Can you change a god by changing the story? Part scholar, part heretic, part revolutionary.

- **The Delta Syndicate** — the Brackish evolving from informal traders into a proper mercantile power as trade increases. They don't care who's fighting. They care about margins.

## Historical Figures in the Transition

- **Vaya** either becomes a legendary figure (if the Song spread) or dies in obscurity (if it didn't). Her songs persist either way — the Rootwalkers remember.

- **Harren** dies during the transition. Whether he dies vindicated or bitter depends on whether trade opened. His forge goes to whoever the player influenced — or nobody, if no apprentice met his standard.

- **Senator Lyris** either achieved her legacy (trade opened) or pivoted to other strategies (trade rejected). She's politically resilient. She always lands on her feet. Whether that's admirable or repulsive depends on your values.

- **Ossa** becomes critical if the unworship path was taken. If not, she remains an eccentric elder in the Ashfield, talking to steam. Either way, she was right about the Ashfield being confused. Whether anyone listened in time is the question.
