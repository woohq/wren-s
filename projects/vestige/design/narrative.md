# VESTIGE — Narrative & Writing Principles

*Every word earned. Every scene hand-written. Every life the canonical story.*

---

## The Standard

The prose must be **Roadwarden-level**. Not gamey. Not "you gain +2 STR." Literary.

> NOT: "You successfully defend the village. +10 reputation."
>
> YES: "The last raider falls face-first into the mud. Your sword arm is shaking — not from exertion, from something that hasn't caught up to your body yet. Behind you, someone is crying. It takes you a moment to realize they're crying because it's over. The village is still standing. So are you. Barely."

The reader should forget they're playing a game. The prose should read like a novel you can't put down — except at every turn, you choose what happens next.

---

## Core Writing Rules

### 1. Every Path Is the Canonical Path

Aureus (Roadwarden): *"I wanted each scene to feel natural, as if it treats the player's path as the 'canonical,' linear, intended story, even though no such thing exists."*

Write every event as if it's THE story. No "meanwhile, you could have..." No sense that a better option existed. The player chose this path. This path is real. The others never happened.

### 2. Show, Don't Tell (Especially Stats)

Never expose mechanics through prose. The player should feel the mechanical change, not read it.

> NOT: "Your Body attunement increases to Focused."
>
> YES: "Something clicks. The next time you lift the hammer, it doesn't feel heavy. It doesn't feel light either. It feels... right. As if your body finally understood what your mind has been trying to teach it for three years."

### 3. Consequences Through Prose, Not Popups

> NOT: "Warning: this choice will make Faction X hostile."
>
> YES: The NPC's face changes. The temperature in the room drops. They say something careful and polite that means everything between you just changed. The player FEELS the consequence in the writing.

### 4. Characters Speak Like People

Not like quest-givers. Not like lore-dumps. Like people who have somewhere else to be.

> NOT: "Greetings, traveler. I am Aldric, High Keeper of the Flame. Our order has guarded the Sacred Fire for three hundred years. Recently, dark forces have—"
>
> YES: "Aldric doesn't look up from his desk. 'You're the one from the north.' It's not a question. 'Sit down. Don't touch the candles.' He finishes writing something, blows the ink dry, and finally meets your eyes. He looks exhausted. 'I need to ask you something, and I need you to not lie to me. Can you do that?'"

### 5. Fluff Is Structural

Not every interaction needs mechanical weight. Some moments exist to make the player feel something — to build atmosphere, to deepen a character, to let the player express their character's personality.

> "The innkeeper asks if you want honey in your tea. There's no mechanical benefit. But saying yes means your character has a preference. Saying 'whatever you have' means they're easygoing. Saying no means they're direct. These micro-choices build a person."

This is Roadwarden's "vibe check" principle. The choice doesn't change the story. It changes how the player relates to their character.

### 6. Violence Has Weight

Inspired by Fear and Hunger: violence is condemnation, not spectacle.

> NOT: "You strike the bandit for 12 damage. Critical hit!"
>
> YES: "Your blade catches him across the forearm and he drops the knife. He's younger than you expected — eighteen, maybe nineteen. He clutches the wound and looks at you with an expression you'll remember for a long time. Not anger. Surprise. As if he genuinely didn't think this would happen to him."

Every combat should have a human cost visible in the prose. Enemies are people (mostly). Killing them should mean something.

## The Anti-Ad-Libs Implementation

### What We're Avoiding

```
BAD (template):
"[CHARACTER] walks to [LOCATION] where [NPC] is waiting.
'I need your help,' says [NPC]. 'The [THREAT] is coming.'"
```

The player sees this pattern after 2-3 events and the immersion breaks.

### What We're Doing Instead

**Scene-level authoring for specific contexts.** Each event is written for a narrow combination of archetype + relationship type + situation. Multiple variants exist. The system picks the one that matches.

**Example: "Mentor asks for help" beat**

Variant for mentor = military commander, archetype = warrior:
> "Harren finds you at the training ground at dawn. He's carrying his old shield — the one with the dent from Blackwater. He never carries it unless something is wrong. 'I'm calling in the favor,' he says. No preamble. No explanation. Harren doesn't explain. 'Three days from now, I need you at the northern ridge. Bring your sword, not your questions.'"

Variant for mentor = scholarly priest, archetype = apprentice:
> "Sister Vael leaves a note under your door. Not the usual lesson assignments — no seal, no formal address. Just three words in her cramped handwriting: 'Come. Bring candles.' You find her in the archive basement, surrounded by texts she shouldn't have access to. She doesn't greet you. 'Close the door,' she says. 'I've found something. And I wish I hadn't.'"

**Zero structural overlap.** Different prose, different rhythm, different character voice, different mood. Both are "mentor asks for help." Neither reads like the other.

### The Name Problem

Names are generated from culture-appropriate pools and embedded deeply into the prose. Not slotted at the last minute — woven in from the start.

The FIRST time a character's name appears should be a moment:
> "She tells you her name like she's testing whether you deserve it. 'Mira.' One syllable. Hard consonant. She watches your face to see if you flinch."

After that, the name is used naturally in prose — in dialogue from other characters, in internal thought, in descriptions. It should feel like the scene was written FOR this specific person.

## Tone by Life Phase

### Childhood — Warm, Intimate, Small

The world is a house, a street, a parent's hand. Even in a cruel world, childhood has pockets of safety. The prose should feel protected — like reading under a blanket.

> "Your mother hums while she cooks. You don't know the song — she says it's from before you were born, from a place she doesn't talk about. The melody is simple. Two notes up, one note down, repeat. Someday you'll hear it again, in a place you've never been, and your chest will hurt for a reason you can't name."

### Adolescence — Electric, Uncertain, Raw

Everything is too much. Too loud, too bright, too emotional. The prose should feel like a nerve ending.

> "You're angry and you don't know why. The anger has no shape — it fills every container. The sky is too wide. The forge is too hot. Seri is too close and too far away at the same time. You break a plate at dinner and your father doesn't say anything and that's worse than if he'd shouted."

### Adulthood — Complex, Weighted, Deliberate

Choices matter and you know it. The prose should feel like carrying something heavy — not crushing, but present.

> "The treaty is three pages. You've read it four times. Each time you find another clause that means something different from what it says. Aldric watches you read. He knows you've found the trap — you can tell by the way he's not smiling. 'Everything in there is fair,' he says. 'That's the problem.'"

### Late Life — Reflective, Bittersweet, Heavy with Meaning

The character has a history now. Every choice carries the weight of all previous choices. The prose should feel like looking at a long road behind you.

> "You sit in the forge — your father's forge, your forge, soon to be no one's forge — and listen to the coals breathe. Forty-seven years. You've made ten thousand blades, and you remember every one that mattered. The one for the war. The one for Seri, who left. The one you buried with your father. The one you're making now, slowly, for no reason except that your hands still know how."

## Writing Workflow

### How to Author an Event

1. **Context first:** Who is this for? Which archetype, which traits, which life phase, which world state?
2. **The moment:** What's happening? What's the emotional core? What makes this scene MATTER?
3. **The choices:** 2-4 options. Each must feel real — no obviously right answer. Trait-gated options where appropriate.
4. **The consequences:** What changes? In the world, in relationships, in the character? Show it through prose.
5. **The test:** Read it aloud. Does it sound like a novel? Or a game manual?

### How to Author a Relationship Scene

1. **Which type?** (childhood friend, mentor, rival, etc.)
2. **Which beat?** (introduction, bonding, critical moment, callback)
3. **Write 3-5 variants** for different archetype combinations
4. **Each variant must stand completely alone** — no shared sentences, no structural template
5. **Include a specific detail** that can be called back later (the bucket at the well, the dented shield, the song she hums)

### The Callback Rule

Every bonding scene should plant ONE specific detail. Later scenes reference that EXACT detail:

**Plant (bonding scene):**
> "He fixes the chair leg without being asked. You watch his hands — careful, precise. He sands the joint until it's smooth, then tests it twice before putting it back. 'There,' he says, like he's apologizing for the chair being broken in the first place."

**Callback (critical moment, 20 events later):**
> "His hands are shaking. These are the hands that fixed the chair — careful, precise, apologetic. Now they're holding a knife, and they can't stop shaking. 'I didn't want this,' he says. You believe him. That's the worst part."

The callback transforms the detail. The chair-fixing hands become the knife-holding hands. The player remembers the earlier scene and feels the distance between then and now.

## Voice Notes for Key Characters

*For the authored world — these are guidelines for when we start writing content.*

**The God (between-lives narration):** Distant, panoramic, slightly melancholy. Sees the world as a web of connections. Uses long sentences that breathe. Never emotional — but the restraint itself is emotional.

**Mortal narration (during lives):** Close, immediate, sensory. Short sentences in combat. Longer sentences in reflection. The character's personality colors the prose — a brave character's narration is direct; a cautious character's narration notices exits.

**NPCs:** Each NPC has a speech pattern shaped by their traits. A terse soldier talks in fragments. A scholarly priest talks in qualifications ("Perhaps, although..."). A child speaks in certainties. No NPC talks like a game character. Every NPC talks like a person.

## Meta-Principles (About the Craft Itself)

These govern how you USE the techniques above. They're about restraint, taste, and knowing when not to write.

### Inspiration Is Not Cosplay

Draw from real history freely. But everything must pass through the lens of this world. Learn the dynamic, the lesson, the human pattern — not the surface details. The reader should feel resonance with reality without being able to point at the source. If someone reads an event and says "that's just X from history," the lens wasn't strong enough.

### Every Technique Has a Saturation Point

Cultural speech patterns, moral complexity, callback details, historical parallels, the anti-ad-libs approach — each is powerful. Each becomes toxic at overuse. The moment the player notices a pattern repeating across cultures or events, the immersion breaks. The system becomes visible. Visible systems feel like games, not worlds.

The discipline: use each technique deliberately, in the specific moment it serves. Then put it away. Variety of technique is itself a technique.

### Asymmetry Is Authenticity

Not every culture should express identity the same way. Not every faction needs the same structural depth. Not every event needs the same number of choices or the same word count. Real worlds are uneven. If every culture has a signature greeting, the greeting system is what players notice. If ONE culture has one and others express identity through different mechanisms entirely, the world feels organic.

Apply this to everything: some relationships develop through dialogue, others through silence. Some events have four choices, others have two, others have none. Some characters are verbose, others barely speak. The unevenness IS the realism.

### Restraint Is the Hardest Skill

The most powerful moments often need the least ornamentation. When the emotional weight is real, plain language hits harder than poetry. When a character dies, "he didn't come back" is stronger than three paragraphs about the void they left. Trust the reader. Trust the moment. Don't decorate grief.

This applies to worldbuilding techniques too. Not every scene needs a moral dilemma. Not every conversation needs subtext. Not every culture needs lore explained. Sometimes a person is just buying bread. Sometimes silence is just silence. The world feels real when it includes the ordinary alongside the extraordinary.
